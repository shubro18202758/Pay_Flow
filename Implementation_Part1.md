## **Forensic Analysis of Catalyst Case Studies**

To engineer a resilient anti-fraud system, the architecture must address the specific vulnerabilities exploited in historical financial crimes. The intelligence model's design is directly informed by the mechanical failures observed in two of the most significant laundering and cyber-heist events of the past two decades.

### **The Danske Bank Estonia Branch Laundering Complex**

Between 2007 and 2015, the Estonia branch of Danske Bank facilitated the laundering of approximately $230 billion in suspicious transactions, primarily originating from Russia, Azerbaijan, Moldova, and Latvia.6 This case highlighted the catastrophic failure of traditional Anti-Money Laundering (AML) transaction monitoring systems, which relied heavily on linear, rule-based risk scoring models.6

The fraudsters utilized non-resident portfolios consisting of shell companies with no legitimate business activity.6 The illicit capital was moved through the classic three-stage laundering process: placement, layering, and integration.6 During the placement stage, illegal funds were injected into the banking system through rapid wire transfers and cash deposits.6 The critical failure of the bank's detection systems occurred during the layering stage, where funds were repeatedly transferred across multiple accounts and foreign banks to obscure their origin.6

A primary mechanism utilized was the circular transaction pattern, where funds were transferred sequentially (e.g., Account A to Account B to Account C, and back to Account A).6 Traditional AML systems, which score transactions in isolation based on static thresholds (such as evaluating if a single transaction exceeds a certain monetary value or originates from a high-risk geographic location), are mathematically blind to these multi-hop topological structures.6 The intelligence model developed in this blueprint mandates the use of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) specifically to identify the structural signatures of these circular flows and money mule networks.6

### **The Bangladesh Bank SWIFT Cyber Heist**

In February 2016, a sophisticated cybercriminal syndicate attempted to steal $951 million from the Bangladesh Bank's account at the Federal Reserve Bank of New York, successfully laundering $81 million through casinos in the Philippines.6 This event demonstrated how vulnerabilities in interbank messaging infrastructure and internal cybersecurity could be exploited to manipulate the very systems designed to track funds.

The attackers spent months conducting reconnaissance via spear-phishing campaigns, eventually deploying malware that compromised the bank's local Society for Worldwide Interbank Financial Telecommunication (SWIFT) terminals.6 The fundamental architectural vulnerability exposed here was the lack of immutable, tamper-evident logging. The deployed malware successfully manipulated the SWIFT system logs, deleted transaction records, altered confirmation messages, and even disabled the physical printers used by bank staff to review daily SWIFT confirmations.6 Because the logs existed in a mutable, centralized database, the attackers easily covered their tracks, delaying detection until a simple spelling error (writing "Shalika Fandation" instead of "Shalika Foundation") triggered a manual review by routing banks.6

To counter advanced persistent threats that compromise internal logging mechanisms, modern fraud architectures must incorporate applied blockchain technology. By anchoring critical system states, alerts, and transaction hashes into a lightweight, local distributed ledger, the system establishes a cryptographically secure audit trail that guarantees data integrity even if the primary network is breached.9

## **Edge Hardware Architecture and VRAM Optimization Paradigm**

Deploying a comprehensive intelligence orchestrator running a 9-billion-parameter LLM locally requires severe architectural discipline, particularly when constrained by the hardware specifications of a Lenovo Legion 7i laptop.1 The NVIDIA RTX 4070 mobile GPU features an Ada Lovelace architecture, providing exceptional compute density and 4th-generation Tensor Cores highly capable of running inference workflows.17 However, the strict physical limitation of 8GB of GDDR6 Video Random Access Memory (VRAM) dictates the entire software design.1

### **The Physics of Local LLM Inference**

The Qwen 3.5 9B model utilizes an advanced hybrid architecture that fuses linear attention via Gated Delta Networks with a sparse Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) design.18 The model consists of 32 layers, operating on an ![][image1] hidden layout.18 While the MoE architecture ensures that only a fraction of the total parameters are activated during any single forward pass—thereby drastically increasing inference speed and reducing computational overhead—the entire model weight must still reside within the VRAM to prevent the system from aggressively swapping to slower system RAM.18

In uncompressed 16-bit floating-point precision (FP16), a 9B parameter model requires approximately 18GB of VRAM solely to hold the weights, immediately causing an Out-Of-Memory (OOM) failure on an 8GB GPU.21 Therefore, rigorous quantization strategies must be applied. Utilizing a 4-bit quantization format, such as the Generalized Graphic Unified Format (GGUF) Q4\_K\_M or Q4\_K\_XL, shrinks the model's physical footprint to approximately 5.2GB to 5.6GB.2

### **Key-Value (KV) Cache Management**

Loading the quantized model leaves approximately 2.4GB of VRAM available for the operating system, the machine learning models, the graph analytics engine, and the LLM's Key-Value (KV) cache.2 The KV cache stores previous token computations to prevent recalculating the entire sequence during auto-regressive generation. Qwen 3.5 9B boasts a native context length of 262,144 tokens, but fully utilizing this window is mathematically impossible on this hardware.18 For an 8B to 9B model, expanding the context window to 32,000 tokens can consume up to 5GB of VRAM for the KV cache alone, while a 128,000-token context demands nearly 20GB.2

To facilitate the agentic orchestration required for fraud analysis without crashing the application, the architecture must enforce strict KV cache quantization.2 By configuring the Ollama engine to utilize 8-bit (q8\_0) or 4-bit (q4\_0) KV cache precision, the memory consumption per token is halved.2 Even with this optimization, the prompt engineering framework must artificially cap the context window to a safe maximum of 16,000 to 24,000 tokens, preserving the remaining VRAM for concurrent analytical processes.2

### **Windows 11/12 Operating System Optimizations**

The Lenovo Legion 7i environment must be optimized at the operating system level to support this intensive dual-workload of LLM inference and data processing.24 Windows Hardware-Accelerated GPU Scheduling (HAGS) must be enabled within the system's graphics settings.24 HAGS offloads high-frequency scheduling tasks from the CPU to a dedicated scheduling processor on the GPU, significantly reducing latency and improving resource allocation when the system rapidly switches contexts between evaluating graph structures and generating LLM tokens.24 Furthermore, ensuring that tools like ONNX Runtime (ORT) and DirectML are utilized will allow the traditional machine learning layers to execute natively and efficiently within the Windows environment.26

## **Algorithmic Foundations of the Intelligence Model**

### **Real-Time Behavioral Analytics and Probability Scoring**

The primary ingestion layer must process high-velocity transaction streams, utilizing traditional but highly optimized machine learning to filter out definitive non-fraudulent activity.6 This layer relies on feature engineering to detect abnormal transaction frequencies, widely known as velocity checks.6 Behavioral analytics profiles typical user login times, geographic locations, and device fingerprints to establish a baseline.6 When a transaction deviates from this profile, the system calculates a probabilistic risk score.6

Modern implementations favor gradient boosting frameworks or deep neural networks that handle the severe class imbalance inherent in financial datasets.6 The AI models estimate the probability of a fraudulent event given the extracted features, defined as ![][image2].6 By deploying these models via GPU-accelerated libraries, the system can execute low-latency inference on the RTX 4070, reserving the heavier computational load for transactions that cross a predefined risk threshold.12

### **Spatial-Temporal Graph Analytics**

When fraudsters deploy synthetic identities or operate through intricate shell companies, individual transactions may not violate probabilistic thresholds.6 To uncover these coordinated campaigns, the intelligence model must represent the financial ecosystem as a multi-relational graph.6 In this formulation, nodes represent bank accounts, entities, or device IDs, while edges represent the transactions and relationships between them.6

A transaction graph allows the application of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs), which have proven vastly superior to traditional tabular machine learning in detecting systemic fraud.7 Architectures such as Heterogeneous Graph Neural Networks (HetGNN) or Temporal Graph Networks (TGN) can ingest the structural signature of a network.27 GNNs operate by passing messages between neighboring nodes, aggregating feature information to create low-dimensional embeddings.7 This enables the system to identify complex typologies, such as money mule networks—characterized by a star-like topology where multiple victim accounts funnel funds into a single node—and circular transactions designed to artificially inflate trading volumes or obscure illicit origins.6

## **Cryptographic Integrity and Blockchain Implementations**

The integration of distributed ledger technologies serves a dual purpose in this architecture: ensuring the absolute integrity of the investigative audit trail and preserving the privacy of the underlying data.9

### **Immutable Local Audit Trails**

The core defense against internal sabotage or sophisticated malware—such as the mechanisms used to disable printers and delete logs during the Bangladesh Bank heist—is the deployment of an embedded, lightweight blockchain.6 Rather than relying on a centralized database susceptible to SQL injection or credential compromise, the system anchors critical events into a cryptographic ledger.9

When the machine learning layer flags a transaction, or the GNN identifies a suspicious subgraph, these events are hashed, timestamped, and sequentially linked into blocks.10 Because each block contains the cryptographic hash of the preceding block, retroactive manipulation is mathematically impossible without invalidating the entire subsequent chain.9 In a local deployment, this does not require a massive, energy-intensive consensus mechanism like Proof-of-Work; instead, a permissioned, lightweight protocol can operate continuously in the background, consuming minimal VRAM and CPU cycles while guaranteeing data integrity.9

### **Zero-Knowledge Proofs (ZKPs) and Smart Contract Interventions**

To align with stringent financial privacy regulations while still enabling deep analytical investigations, the architecture incorporates Zero-Knowledge Proofs (ZKPs).14 ZKPs are cryptographic protocols that allow one party (the prover) to prove to another party (the verifier) that a specific statement is true, without revealing any extraneous information.15 For example, the system can verify that a transaction originates from a fully compliant, non-sanctioned account without exposing the user's underlying personally identifiable information (PII) or entire transaction history to the broader analytical network.14 Modern non-interactive ZKPs, such as zk-SNARKs, are highly efficient and can be executed rapidly within the intelligence model's pipeline.36

Furthermore, the blockchain layer introduces the capability for deterministic, automated responses via smart contracts.9 If the agentic orchestrator determines a critical threat level based on the convergence of ML probability, graph topology, and textual anomalies, it can interact with a smart contract circuit breaker.38 This self-executing code can instantaneously halt transactions, freeze localized account networks, or pause interbank routing, completely neutralizing the threat before funds exit the custody of the institution.38

## **Advanced Fine-Tuning and GPU Acceleration Strategy**

While the foundational prompt sequence establishes a highly capable inference and orchestration architecture, the dynamic nature of financial crime requires the intelligence model to adapt to newly discovered adversarial tactics. Fraudsters continuously evolve their methodologies to evade static detection parameters.6 Consequently, the Qwen 3.5 9B orchestrator must be subjected to periodic fine-tuning to assimilate emerging threat intelligence.44

### **Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) Methodologies**

Executing a full-parameter fine-tuning regimen on a 9-billion-parameter model demands computational resources vastly exceeding the capabilities of an 8GB VRAM GPU. To achieve model adaptation on the Lenovo Legion 7i, Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) methodologies are mandatory.44

The architecture must leverage Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) or its quantized variant, QLoRA. This technique freezes the pre-trained weights of the core model and injects highly efficient, trainable rank decomposition matrices into the Transformer's attention layers.44 By utilizing QLoRA, the base model remains compressed in a 4-bit precision state, while only the lightweight LoRA adapters undergo training updates in 16-bit precision.44 This hybrid precision approach reduces the fine-tuning memory overhead to approximately 5GB to 6GB of VRAM, making continuous learning entirely viable on the RTX 4070\.44

### **Enhancing Logical Reasoning via GRPO**

Traditional Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)—where the model learns to mimic provided input-output data pairs—is insufficient for enhancing the complex, multi-hop reasoning required for investigating intricate fraud topologies.44 To significantly improve the model's "Thinking Mode" and analytical capabilities, the architecture should incorporate Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO).23

GRPO represents a paradigm shift toward reinforcement learning from human (or systemic) feedback, enabling the model to optimize its decision-making processes rather than merely predicting the next token.44 By utilizing GRPO frameworks optimized for consumer hardware in 2026, the fraud analyzer can be trained to systematically reason through graph traversals, rewarding the LLM for successfully identifying the correct root node of a simulated money laundering ring.44
